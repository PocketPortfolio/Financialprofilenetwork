## Abstract

In the realm of database management, ensuring data consistency while minimizing latency is a paramount concern, particularly in distributed systems with read replicas. This research delves into the performance trade-offs between consistency and latency in configurations employing read replicas. By systematically analyzing various database setups, we aim to elucidate how different consistency levels affect read latency and overall system performance. Our findings seek to guide database administrators and system architects in optimizing their configurations for the best balance between data accuracy and response times.

## Methodology

The research employed a quantitative approach, using a combination of simulation and real-world experimentation to evaluate the impact of consistency levels on latency in read replica setups. We selected a representative sample of popular database systems, including PostgreSQL, MySQL, and MongoDB, each offering distinct mechanisms for managing read replicas and consistency. 

For each database system, we established a primary-replica configuration with varying numbers of read replicas. We then simulated workloads characterized by different read-write ratios, measuring read latency and consistency levels across these setups. Consistency levels were adjusted from eventual to strong consistency to observe their impact on read latency. The metrics collected included average read latency, percentage of read requests meeting consistency requirements, and the throughput of the system.

## Key Findings

Our analysis revealed several key insights into the performance trade-offs between consistency and latency in read replica configurations:

- **Eventual Consistency vs. Strong Consistency**: Systems configured for eventual consistency exhibited significantly lower read latencies compared to those enforcing strong consistency. However, this came at the cost of occasional stale reads.
- **Impact of Read Replica Count**: Increasing the number of read replicas generally improved read throughput but had a diminishing return on latency reduction, particularly under strong consistency requirements.
- **Differences Among Database Systems**: MongoDB's read preference tags and PostgreSQL's synchronous replication options allowed for more granular control over consistency and latency trade-offs compared to MySQL's more binary approach.
