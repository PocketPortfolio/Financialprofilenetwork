## Abstract

In the realm of digital data transfer, file upload performance is pivotal for user experience and system efficiency. Two prevalent methods are multipart uploads and streaming. Multipart uploads break files into smaller parts, making them easier to manage and transmit. Streaming, on the other hand, allows a continuous flow of data, reducing latency and improving speed. This research evaluates these methods in terms of performance, focusing on upload speed, reliability, and resource utilization. By comparing multipart and streaming uploads, the study aims to identify which method offers superior performance for modern applications.

## Methodology

This study employed an empirical approach to evaluate the performance of multipart and streaming file uploads. We conducted controlled experiments using a variety of file sizes, ranging from small text files to large video files, to test upload efficiency across different network conditions. The experiments were structured to simulate real-world scenarios using HTTP protocols, specifically HTTP/1.1, HTTP/2, and HTTP/3, to observe how each protocol affects upload performance.

Data collection involved measuring upload time, resource consumption (CPU and memory usage), and error rates. Each method's performance was assessed under varying conditions, such as fluctuating bandwidth and latency, to ensure a comprehensive analysis. Tools such as Wireshark and Postman were utilized to monitor network traffic and analyze data packets, providing insights into the efficiency of each upload method.

## Key Findings

The study revealed several critical insights into the performance of multipart and streaming file uploads:

1. **Upload Speed**: Streaming consistently outperformed multipart uploads in terms of speed, particularly for larger files. This is due to the continuous data flow in streaming, which minimizes overhead and reduces latency, resulting in faster upload times.

2. **Resource Utilization**: Multipart uploads exhibited higher CPU and memory usage compared to streaming. The process of dividing files into smaller parts and reassembling them on the server side was resource-intensive, particularly for large files. Streaming, by contrast, maintained consistent resource usage, making it more efficient for systems with limited resources.

3. **Reliability and Error Rates**: Multipart uploads showed a higher error rate under unstable network conditions. The fragmentation of data increases the likelihood of packet loss, leading to incomplete uploads. Streaming demonstrated greater reliability, maintaining integrity by continuously adjusting to network fluctuations.

4. **Protocol Impact**: HTTP/3 significantly improved the performance of both methods, with reduced latency and faster recovery from packet loss compared to HTTP/1.1 and HTTP/2. The benefits were more pronounced for streaming uploads, which leveraged HTTP/3’s advanced features to optimize data transfer.

## Future Trends

As technology advances, the efficiency of file uploads will continue to evolve. Emerging trends include the integration of machine learning to optimize upload paths and reduce latency further. Additionally, with the increasing adoption of HTTP/3, we can expect even greater improvements in data transfer speeds and reliability. The rise of edge computing will also play a significant role, as processing data closer to the source can significantly reduce upload times and enhance performance.

## Verdict

The choice between multipart and streaming uploads depends on specific application requirements and resource availability. For applications dealing with large files and requiring high speed and reliability, streaming is the superior choice. However, in environments where resources are abundant and network conditions stable, multipart uploads may suffice. Overall, as HTTP/3 becomes more widespread, streaming is likely to become the preferred method due to its efficiency and adaptability.
