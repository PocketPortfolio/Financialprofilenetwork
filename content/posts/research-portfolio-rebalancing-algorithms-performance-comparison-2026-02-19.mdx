## Abstract

Portfolio rebalancing is a critical process in portfolio management, aimed at realigning the weightings of a portfolio of assets. It involves buying and selling positions to maintain a desired level of asset allocation or risk. This research report delves into the performance comparison of various portfolio rebalancing algorithms, highlighting key findings in terms of efficiency, cost implications, and risk-adjusted returns. By analyzing various algorithmic strategies, including periodic rebalancing, threshold-based rebalancing, and optimization models like the Mean-Variance Optimization (MVO), this study uncovers the trade-offs each method presents. The analysis leverages a combination of historical market data, simulated portfolio scenarios, and academic models to benchmark algorithm performance. The report further discusses the significance of these findings for investors and portfolio managers, emphasizing the impact on portfolio performance and risk management.

## Methodology

The research conducted for this report utilized a three-pronged approach: 

1. **Historical Market Data Analysis**: Utilizing raw data from major financial markets over the past decade, this study evaluated the performance of rebalancing algorithms across various market conditions.
2. **Simulated Portfolio Scenarios**: A range of portfolio scenarios was created to assess how different rebalancing strategies respond to market changes, including volatile and stable periods.
3. **Academic Models and Benchmarks**: The study incorporated established financial models, such as the Mean-Variance Optimization model and the Efficient Frontier concept, to evaluate the theoretical underpinnings of each rebalancing strategy.

## Key Findings

The research illuminated several critical insights:

- **Performance Efficiency**: Periodic rebalancing strategies, while simpler, often underperform in rapidly changing markets compared to more dynamic strategies like threshold-based rebalancing.
- **Cost Implications**: Threshold-based strategies, although potentially offering better returns, can incur higher transaction costs due to more frequent trades.
- **Risk-Adjusted Returns**: Optimization models, particularly those that incorporate the Efficient Frontier, as explained in the video "Efficient Frontier and Portfolio Optimization Explained | The Ultimate Guide" by Ryan O'Connell, CFA, FRM, tend to offer superior risk-adjusted returns. However, they require more sophisticated calculations and real-time market data.
