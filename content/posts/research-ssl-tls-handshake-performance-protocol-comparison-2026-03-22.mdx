## Abstract

The Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) and Transport Layer Security (TLS) protocols are fundamental to establishing secure connections over the internet. The handshake process, which initiates these secure connections, is critical in determining the overall performance and efficiency of the protocols. This research report provides an in-depth analysis of SSL/TLS handshake performance by comparing different protocol versions. The goal is to offer insights into which versions deliver optimal performance, considering factors such as connection speed and resource utilization.

## Methodology

To assess the performance of the SSL/TLS handshake, we conducted a series of experiments that measured handshake duration and resource consumption across various protocol versions, including SSL 3.0, TLS 1.0, TLS 1.2, and TLS 1.3. The tests were performed in a controlled environment using a standardized set of hardware configurations to ensure consistency. Each protocol underwent multiple iterations to account for variability in network conditions. Metrics such as handshake time, CPU usage, and memory consumption were recorded and analyzed.

## Key Findings

1. **Handshake Time**: TLS 1.3 demonstrated the fastest handshake times, consistently completing handshakes in under 100 ms. This is largely due to its streamlined handshake process, which requires fewer round trips compared to previous versions.

2. **Resource Utilization**: TLS 1.3 also showed reduced CPU and memory usage compared to its predecessors. The efficiency of TLS 1.3 is attributed to its simplified design and improved cryptographic techniques.

3. **Compatibility and Adoption**: Despite the superior performance of TLS 1.3, TLS 1.2 remains widely used due to its extensive compatibility with older systems and applications. However, the trend towards adopting TLS 1.3 is accelerating as more systems upgrade to support it.

4. **Security Enhancements**: In addition to performance benefits, TLS 1.3 offers significant security improvements, such as the elimination of obsolete and vulnerable cryptographic algorithms.
